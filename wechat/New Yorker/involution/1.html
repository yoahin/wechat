China’s “Involuted” Generation

A new word has entered the popular lexicon to describe feelings of burnout, ennuiⒹ, and despair.

By Yi-Ling Liu

May 14, 2021

In many ways, China’s affliction of involution is no different from America’s cutthroat meritocracy. But China’s crisis is unique in the severity of its myopiaⒹand its methods of entrapmentⒹ.Illustration by Xiao Hua Yang

Last September, a student at Beijing’s élitefirst-class Tsinghua University was caught on video㋡ riding his bike at night and working on a laptop propped on his handlebars. The footage circulated on Chinese social media, and shortly afterward more photos[1] of other Tsinghua students—slumped载倒Ⓓat cafeteria tables, buried under stacks of textbooks—appeared online. Commentators proceeded to roastcriticize the insane work ethic on display and tag the students as part of a rising generation of “involuted” young people. The cyclist became a meme[2] —“Tsinghua’s Involuted King卷王”—and a flurry ofsuddenly-emerging blog posts on Chinese social media criticized the “involution of élite education,” while an article published by the state-run Xinhua News Agency dissectedanalyzed the “involution of college students.” By the time winter arrived, the idea of involution had spread to all corners of Chinese society.

The American anthropologist Clifford Geertz❉ helped popularize the term in his book “Agricultural Involution,”[3] from 1963, in which he analyzed Java’s爪哇 economic response to population growth and Dutch colonial rule. Geertz’s theory of involution holdsargues that a greater input (an increase in labor) does not yield proportional output (more crops and innovation). Instead, a society involutes. The Chinese term for involution, neijuan, which is made up of the characters for “inside” and “rolling,” suggests a process that curls inward, ensnaring its participants within what the anthropologist Xiang Biao has described as an “endless cycle of self-flagellation.”[4] Involution is “the experience of being locked in competition that one ultimately knows is meaningless,” Biao told me. It is acceleration without a destination, progress without a purpose, Sisyphus西西弗斯❉ spinning the wheels of a perpetual-motion PelotonⒹ❶

Clifford Geertz

Clifford James Geertz (/ɡɜːrts/; August 23, 1926 – October 30, 2006) was an American anthropologist who is remembered mostly for his strong support for and influence on the practice of symbolic anthropology, and who was considered "for three decades...the single most influential cultural anthropologist in the United States." He served until his death as professor emeritus at the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton.

He taught or held fellowships at a number of schools before joining the faculty of the anthropology department at the University of Chicago in 1960. In this period Geertz expanded his focus on Indonesia to include both Java and Bali and produced three books, including Religion of Java (1960), Agricultural Involution (1963), and Peddlers and Prince (also 1963).

Sisyphus

In Greek mythology Sisyphus or Sisyphos (/ˈsɪsɪfəs/; Ancient Greek: Σίσυφος Sísyphos) was the founder and king of Ephyra (now known as Corinth). He was punished for cheating death twice by being forced to roll an immense boulder up a hill only for it to roll down every time it neared the top, repeating this action for eternity. Through the classical influence on modern culture, tasks that are both laborious and futile are therefore described as Sisyphean (/sɪsɪˈfiːən/).
