When my father was a boy, he was sent to be an apprentice at a temple somewhere in Nara奈良市 . The understanding, presumablyvery likely , was that he would be adopted into the Nara priest’s family. However, after his apprenticeship he returned home to Kyoto. This was ostensiblyon the surface because the cold had adversely affected㋡ his health, but the main reason seems to have been that he couldn’t adjust to the new environment. After returning home, he lived, as before, as his parents’ son. But I get the feeling that the experience remained with him, as a deep emotional scar. I can’t point to any particular evidence of this, but there was something about him that made me feel that way.

❉WikipediA

Seizan (西山) is the name of the branch of Jōdo-shū Buddhism (净土宗) that was founded by Hōnen's (法然) disciple, Shōkū (証空). Shōkū often went by the name Seizan as well, however the name derives from the western mountains of Kyoto where Shōkū often dwelt.

As a branch of Jōdo, the central practice is devotion to Amida Buddha (阿彌陀佛), and recitation of the nembutsu (念佛). Shōkū coined the term shiraki no nembutsu, which refers to a practice where one devotes themselves to intense study of Buddhist literature, and then recites the nembutsu with deep, sincere faith. The idea behind shiraki no nembutsu is to demonstrate that in the age of Mappo (末法時期), people cannot achieve Enlightenment on their own, and so they should rely on the compassion of Amida Buddha.
